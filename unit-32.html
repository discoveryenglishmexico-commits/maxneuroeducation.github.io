Is Social Media Creating a Lonely Planet?

Exploring whether updating social networks affects loneliness and human connection.

📖 Reading: The Digital Connection Paradox

The Staggering Numbers

A new study explores whether or not updating social networks affects loneliness.

📊 Daily Social Media Activity:
• Over 400 million Tweets published on Twitter every day
• 48 hours of video uploaded on YouTube on a daily basis
• LinkedIn recently announced that it had reached the 200 million-user mark
• The numbers are only getting bigger

But, even with these staggering numbers across some of the world's largest and most important social networks, nothing compares in size, volume and girth to the people's republic of Facebook.

🌍 Facebook's Population:
• Over one billion people on earth
• Represents 12% of all those who inhabit this planet
• Facebook is becoming more of its own Internet where people become the nodes of a global social network and relationships form the interconnections between them

The Egosystem: You at the Center

This isn't a discussion about Facebook though. For many, Facebook is at the heart of what I refer to as an egosystem, a network where people literally place themselves at the center of their own digital universe. They surround themselves with the people, businesses, organizations, and artists they find most interesting to follow and also engage.

⚠️ Important Distinction:
Don't confuse the egosystem with egotism however. Because people are at the center of their online experiences they in theory control what they see and in turn how they act and react. The question is though whether or not people who update social networks contribute to self-centeredness or if there's another reason behind it.

In the very least, people are much more connected and informed nowadays. Facebook and other social networks are affecting the way people learn, communicate, and share. But we're learning that when people do share something in social networks, they may do so with a form of hope or even confidence that people will in real-time and due time stop, notice, and respond.

Take a moment to consider why you post online? Do you do so because it's simply self-expression? Do you share what you're doing to prompt a reaction? Is it cathartic? Whatever it is, it's important to understand as it's setting the stage for everyday social networking.

The Research: Does Posting Reduce Loneliness?

That's when I stumbled across a research paper, "Does Posting Facebook Status Updates Increase or Decrease Loneliness?"

✅ Key Finding:
Self-centeredness? Almost immediately, the researchers found that individuals who regularly update Facebook reduced the sensation of loneliness. Why? Believe it or not people felt more connected and in touch with friends on a daily basis.

A research explored communication types and the closeness of relationships during a student's transition to college. They learned that computer-mediated communication emerged as more important than phone calls for sustaining friendships.

People believe that sooner or later, their friends will read the update and that's enough to satisfy or at least defer solitary sentiment.

Digital Relationships: A New Definition?

Perhaps in the back of your mind, you're wondering or possibly scoffing at the idea of a digital relationship. There's just no way that a connection on a social network or the act of updating status boxes can replace the importance of a physical connection and real-world conversations right?

🔄 Generational Differences:
Like online privacy before it, the significance or even definition of relationships may in fact be different across generations. It doesn't make it wrong, but it is indeed interesting and worthy of study and understanding.

While social networking is something that is a learned behavior among matures, boomers and Gen Xers, engaging with friends across laptop, tablet and mobile screens is completely natural to Millennials and Gen Z. It's simply in their DNA.

Now, how social networking overall affects long-term personality traits, such as narcissism, empathy, self-esteem, etc., will take further study. But in this particular case, social networking does indeed carry positive connotations. Whether you agree or disagree, we found that closeness, or the appearance of it, is a direct result of updating social status. So for now, it can be said that online interaction maintains and cultivates real world relationships.
