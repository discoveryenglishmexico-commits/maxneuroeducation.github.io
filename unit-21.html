your trip to work
• the morning commute
• the evening commute
• have a [long / short] commute
• How long is your commute?
• I have a [30-minute] commute.

To commute (verb)

travel to work
• commute from
• commute to
• commute by (type of transportation)

A commuter (noun)

person who travels to work every day

A commuter rail (noun)

the system of passenger trains that take people to and from work
• take the commuter rail
• use the commuter rail
• buy a commuter rail pass

Carpool (noun, verb)

(the act of) sharing a car ride to and from work every day along with the travel costs
• be in a carpool with (whom)
• carpool with (whom)

To telecommute

work for a company from a home office via phone and the Internet
• have the option of telecommuting

📚 Grammar Review

Conditionals

What are conditionals in English grammar? Sometimes we call them 'if clauses'. They describe the result of something that might happen (in the present or future) or might have happened but didn't (in the past). They are made using different English verb tenses.

There are four kinds:

Conditional TypeStructureExample Zero Conditionalif + present simple, ... present simpleIf you heat water to 100 degrees, it boils. First Conditionalif + present simple, ... will + infinitiveIf it rains tomorrow, we'll go to the cinema. Second Conditionalif + past simple, ... would + infinitiveIf I had a lot of money, I would travel around the world. Third Conditionalif + past perfect, ... would + have + past participleIf I had gone to bed early, I would have caught the train.
Note: We can put 'if' at the beginning, or in the middle. It doesn't matter at all.

Explanation of "Nevertheless"

English learners are often confused by "nevertheless" and "however," and for good reason! You can always replace "nevertheless" with "however," but you cannot always replace "however" with "nevertheless."

"Nevertheless" is a transition. Like all transitions, "nevertheless" links two clauses, showing a logical relationship between them. "Nevertheless" indicates concession. What is concession? Put simply, a concession is something that is unexpected or surprising.

Consider this statement:

Jane lived in Tokyo for ten years.

So, given that Jane lived in Japan for a long time, what would be unexpected or surprising?

  • Jane has never been to Peru. (Not a surprise - Japan and Peru are not close together)
  • Jane can't speak any Japanese. (A SURPRISE! We would expect her to speak at least some Japanese)
Example: Jane lived in Tokyo for ten years; nevertheless, she can't speak any Japanese.

"However" can indicate both concession AND contrast, so you can use "however" anywhere you can use "nevertheless."
