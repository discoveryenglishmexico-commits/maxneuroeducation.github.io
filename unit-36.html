Today's Women

Breaking Barriers: The Evolution of Women's Rights and Roles in Modern Society

📖 Reading: The Journey to Equality

Equal Rights: Reality vs. Perception

Today's women are considered to have equal rights; but, is it really true? Old stereotypes still exist in many things. Women have worked hard for the rights that exist. They have made huge strides in changing the way society looks at them from the past.

In the past, women were to stay at home, take care of the house chores, the cooking, the baking, the laundry, and teach the children by raising them with chores, discipline, and help with their homework. Husbands were expected to come home from work, have dinner at the table and the house neat and clean for them.

💼 The Modern Shift:
Times have changed, though. Many women work outside of the home or even in the home for pay. With today's economic status, most families need two paychecks to make ends meet. Other women have a college degree and want to put it to use or continue in their careers. Although family may be important to them, self-improvement and being able to help provide for the family may be of a concern.

With this shift in work situation, many women ask their husbands for help with the housework and the children and are scrutinized for it. It is not always an easy situation for a woman to be married, work, and have a family; but, it is quite often a choice that is made.

The Wage Gap

Although women are supposed to receive equal pay for equal work; this is generally not the case.

📊 Wage Gap Statistics (2000):
• White women: earned about 75 cents for each dollar white men earned
• African American women: earned 85 cents for each African American dollar
• Hispanic women: earned 88 cents for each Hispanic male dollar

Women are still stereotyped, many times, in regard to jobs. In many cases, a man is chosen over a woman in a particular field.

Stereotypes in Language and Media

Other stereotypes occur in language and in the media. Many textbooks, magazines, and other printed materials are written as "he," "him," or "man" when the intention is to be generic, such as "he/she," "him/her," or "man/woman." Other language intricacies also devalue women.

📺 Media Representation:
In the media, women are still portrayed in television commercials, shows, and movies as the homemaker, housewife, and mother. Some have branched out to include women as a working woman; but, usually only if she is displayed as being single.

In a majority of commercials, women are still in the stereotypical roles. How often do you see a man advertising a cleaning product? A woman may be shown to advertise car parts; but, this is generally done in a sexual and seductive manner not as if she were knowledgeable about an engine.

Women's Contributions to Society

Women are important in our society. They are nurses, teachers, lawyers, police officers, jailers, army soldiers, writers, sales clerks, managers, accountants, business leaders, and so much more. Women make a difference. Women get involved. Women volunteer more than men do.

🌟 The Power of Volunteering:
Much of what has been accomplished in communities is due to the women that volunteer for different groups. These women get things done. It could be part of their nurturing instinct to hold close what is important to them and work for a better world.

The Balancing Act

Women juggle many things in everyday life. For those who are married, or have children, it is even more of a balancing act.

⚖️ The Stress Factor:
According to experts, "Not surprisingly, given the relatively low levels of support women receive for juggling family, personal, and employment obligations, employed wives have less time for themselves and less leisure than employed husbands do, so they experience more stress."

Equality between men and women has not been an easy road to travel. It has not ended yet. The solution will not be something that happens overnight. But, each little stride that is taken is one step closer to a better world for women of today and of the future.

The journey continues. Every step matters.
