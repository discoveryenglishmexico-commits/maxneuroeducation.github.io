🎭 Reading: Opera

What is Opera?

Opera refers to a dramatic art form, originating in Europe, in which the emotional content is conveyed to the audience as much through music, both vocal and instrumental, as it is through the lyrics. By contrast, in musical theater an actor's dramatic performance is primary, and the music plays a lesser role. The drama in opera is presented using the primary elements of theater such as scenery, costumes, and acting. However, the words of the opera, or libretto, are sung rather than spoken. The singers are accompanied by a musical ensemble ranging from a small instrumental ensemble to a full symphonic orchestra.

💿 Critical Thinking: Illegal Downloads

The Reality of Digital Downloads

Who buys Blurays, DVDs or CDs these days? Of course, many download music through iTunes or Amazon, legally purchasing a data copy of the work. However, other individuals have quit making any purchases. Instead they turn to illegal downloads via person-to-person networks on the Internet.

Movie and music companies claim that downloads significantly reduce their profits. Once a movie has been watched, the majority of viewers won't watch it again. In other words, they'll spend their hard-earned cash elsewhere. The same holds true for music. However, the movie and music industry continue to thrive, mostly because people who download other work. In fact, many outside the industry suggest that file sharing has reinvigorated the entertainment industry.

📚 Grammar Review

Whatever / Whenever / Wherever / Whichever / Whoever

These words are used as follows:

WordMeaningExample Whateveranything or everything; regardless of what"Whatever you do, don't forget your grandmother's birthday." Wheneverevery time; at any time; regardless of when"Whenever we organize a picnic it rains." Wherevereverywhere; no matter where; regardless of where"Wherever you go you'll always find someone who speaks English." Whicheverthe person or thing which; no matter which; regardless of which"Wear whichever shoes are the most comfortable." Whoeverthe person who; regardless of who"Whoever comes with you is welcome."

Second Conditional

The second conditional uses the past simple after if, then 'would' and the infinitive:

Structure: (if + past simple, ... would + infinitive)
Note: We use 'were' instead of 'was' with 'I' and 'he/she/it'. This is mostly done in formal writing.

Use 1: Unreal future situations

We can use it to talk about things in the future that are probably not going to be true. Maybe I'm imagining some dream for example.

  • If I won the lottery, I would buy a big house. (I probably won't win)
  • If I met the Queen of England, I would say hello.
  • She would travel all over the world if she were rich.

Use 2: Impossible present situations

We can use it to talk about something in the present which is impossible, because it's not true.

  • If I had his number, I would call him. (I don't have his number now)
  • If I were you, I wouldn't go out with that man.
